Portable 5v supply and charger

I have a bunch of Lipo and Li-ion batteries that I could only use for powering hobby stuff, and I wanted to give them a more practical use. So I made this board where you can plug a battery in and power any USB device that uses 5v and doesn't draw that much current.

Initial design

Here were my design criteria for this board: I wanted it to take 2-6s voltage, which is around 7.4-25.2V, regulate it down to 5V and power a USB port, and have a way to represent voltage so the user knows how much power is left in their battery.

For regulating the voltage, I used a LM7805 linear voltage regulator which can take 7-35V in and output 5V 1.5A. For communicating with the USB port, I used a TPS2513 chip, which connects to the D+ and D- of a USB port and matches the voltage it sees. For example, if you had an iphone that had 2.7V on D+ and 2.0V on D-, the chip would bring its own data pins to the same voltage, allowing the iphone to accept the USB connection. For representing the battery charge, I used a 1/5 voltage divider, a Seeed Xiao SAMD21 microcontroller, and 4 LEDs. The microcontroller detects the voltage of the battery, calculates how many cells the battery has, calculates the average voltage per cell, then turns on a certain amount of LEDs based on the cell voltage. For example, a fully charged Lipo has 4.2V per cell, so 4 LEDs would turn on.

I used KiCAD to design the board, JLCPCB for fabrication, and I bought all the parts from Digikey and hand soldered everything (Bad idea)

Initial results

The board was able to power a variety of things, like charging an iphone and powering arduino boards. However, I ran into a big issue with heat. Because I used a linear regulator, it uses resistance to drop voltage, so all the extra voltage just gets wasted as heat. For example, dropping 12V to 5V at 1A makes 7W of heat! When charging, the board would get really hot and scary, and the voltage regulator would start thermal throttling, limiting my max charging speed only to 5W. I also added a heat sink but it didn't really fix the problem. In hindsight I should have put ground planes on both layers and used way thicker traces to help soak up heat.

Revisions

To try to solve my heat issue, I added an external bypass transistor, using a TIP42C power transistor that could take up to 6A. I also added a 4.7 ohm resistor in series with the voltage regulator. From my understanding, when the voltage regulator draws more than about 200 mA, the voltage drop across the resistor and transistor base reaches 0.9V, allowing current to flow through the transistor, bypassing the regulator.
